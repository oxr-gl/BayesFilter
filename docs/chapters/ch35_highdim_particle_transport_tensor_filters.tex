\chapter[Particle, Transport, And Tensor Filters]{Particle, Transport, Tensor-Train, and Tensor-Network Filters}
\label{ch:bf-highdim-particle-transport-tensor}

\section{Chapter Claim}
\label{sec:bf-highdim-ptt-claim}

Particle, transport, and tensor methods are high-payoff research directions for
high-dimensional nonlinear filtering only when their approximation errors are
measured on the downstream filtering or posterior task.  Particles give
non-Gaussian support but suffer weight degeneracy.  Transport maps can move
samples toward posterior mass but must carry density corrections.  Tensor
trains and tensor networks can compress structured functions or covariance
objects but can fail through rank growth, truncation, positivity, and
normalization errors.  None of these mechanisms removes the curse of
dimensionality by declaration.

\section{Particle Filter Baseline}
\label{sec:bf-highdim-particle-baseline}

The bootstrap particle filter of \citet{gordon1993novel} and related SMC
methods \citep{kitagawa1996montecarlo,arulampalam2002tutorial,doucet2001sequential,chopin2020introduction}
represent the filtering law by a weighted empirical measure
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bf-highdim-particle-measure}
  \widehat\pi_t^N(dx)
  =
  \sum_{i=1}^N \bar w_t^{(i)}\delta_{x_t^{(i)}}(dx),
  \qquad
  \bar w_t^{(i)}
  =
  \frac{w_t^{(i)}}{\sum_{j=1}^N w_t^{(j)}}.
\end{equation}
With a proposal $q_t(x_t\mid x_{t-1},y_t)$, the incremental weight is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bf-highdim-pf-weight}
  \alpha_t^{(i)}
  =
  \frac{g_\theta(y_t\mid x_t^{(i)})
        f_\theta(x_t^{(i)}\mid x_{t-1}^{a(i)})}
       {q_t(x_t^{(i)}\mid x_{t-1}^{a(i)},y_t)}.
\end{equation}
The bootstrap filter is the special case $q_t=f_\theta$, so the incremental
weight is the observation likelihood.  The standard likelihood estimator is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bf-highdim-pf-likelihood-estimator}
  \widehat p_\theta(y_{1:T})
  =
  \prod_{t=1}^T
  \left(\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N w_t^{(i)}\right),
\end{equation}
with the usual SMC caveat that unbiasedness statements concern the likelihood
estimator, not the log likelihood.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Sequential importance resampling filter}
\label{alg:bf-highdim-sir}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Particle count $N$, initial sampler, transition $f_\theta$,
  observation likelihood $g_\theta$, resampling threshold $\tau$.
\State Sample $x_0^{(i)}\sim \mu_\theta$ and set $\bar w_0^{(i)}=1/N$.
\For{$t=1,\ldots,T$}
  \If{$\mathrm{ESS}_{t-1}<\tau N$}
    \State Resample ancestor indices $a(i)$ from $\bar w_{t-1}$.
    \State Reset ancestor weights to $1/N$.
  \Else
    \State Set $a(i)=i$.
  \EndIf
  \State Propagate $x_t^{(i)}\sim f_\theta(\cdot\mid x_{t-1}^{a(i)})$.
  \State Weight $w_t^{(i)}=g_\theta(y_t\mid x_t^{(i)})$.
  \State Normalize weights and record ESS, finite status, and ancestor count.
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The effective sample size diagnostic
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bf-highdim-ess}
  \mathrm{ESS}_t=\frac{1}{\sum_{i=1}^N(\bar w_t^{(i)})^2}
\end{equation}
is a degeneracy signal, not a posterior-accuracy certificate.  In a
high-dimensional observation, small likelihood mismatches compound across
coordinates and the maximum weight can dominate.  The high-dimensional collapse
warnings of \citet{bengtsson2008curse} and \citet{snyder2008obstacles} are
therefore central, not incidental.

\section{Guided And Auxiliary Proposals}
\label{sec:bf-highdim-guided-proposals}

Auxiliary and guided particle filters spend computation before weighting to
choose better ancestors or proposals.  The implementation contract is simple:
if the proposal changes, the density ratio in
\eqref{eq:bf-highdim-pf-weight} must change with it.  A guide can be an EKF
linearization, block IEKF smoother, flow proposal, local Laplace
approximation, or learned policy, but it remains a proposal until the correction
is explicit.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Guided proposal particle step}
\label{alg:bf-highdim-guided-pf}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Ancestor particles, guide state $r_t$, proposal density
  $q_t(\cdot\mid x_{t-1},y_t,r_t)$, transition density $f_\theta$, likelihood
  $g_\theta$.
\For{$i=1,\ldots,N$}
  \State Select ancestor $a(i)$ using the declared auxiliary policy.
  \State Draw $x_t^{(i)}\sim q_t(\cdot\mid x_{t-1}^{a(i)},y_t,r_t)$.
  \State Set $\alpha_t^{(i)}$ using \eqref{eq:bf-highdim-pf-weight}.
\EndFor
\State Normalize, resample if required, and record finite density ratios,
  ESS, and guide residuals.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The practitioner question is not whether the guide looks plausible.  It is
whether the corrected weights avoid collapse and whether the resulting
filtering estimate agrees with a reference on controlled cells.

\section{Transport And Flow Proposals}
\label{sec:bf-highdim-flow-proposals}

Let $z\sim r(z)$ and let $T_\phi$ be an invertible differentiable map with
$x=T_\phi(z)$.  The proposal density induced by the map is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bf-highdim-flow-density}
  q_\phi(x)
  =
  r(T_\phi^{-1}(x))
  \left|\det DT_\phi^{-1}(x)\right|.
\end{equation}
If this proposal is used for filtering, the importance weight must divide by
$q_\phi$.  Normalizing-flow references such as \citet{papamakarios2021flows}
and transport-map MCMC work such as \citet{parno2018transport} support the
change-of-variables and geometry-preconditioning framing, but they do not by
themselves validate a BayesFilter nonlinear SSM proposal.

\begin{proposition}[Transport proposal correction]
\label{prop:bf-highdim-transport-correction}
For any invertible differentiable proposal map with density
\eqref{eq:bf-highdim-flow-density}, importance sampling estimates expectations
under a target density $\gamma(x)/Z$ using weights proportional to
$\gamma(x^{(i)})/q_\phi(x^{(i)})$, provided $q_\phi>0$ wherever $\gamma>0$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For integrable $\varphi$,
$\int \varphi(x)\gamma(x)\,dx
=\int \varphi(x)\gamma(x)q_\phi(x)^{-1}q_\phi(x)\,dx$ on the target support.
Monte Carlo under $q_\phi$ gives the usual self-normalized estimator after
dividing by the estimated normalizing constant.
\end{proof}

The diagnostics are finite inverse map, finite log determinant, finite density
ratio, ESS, support mismatch, and posterior or filtering checks.  A map-training
loss is explanatory only unless the phase plan explicitly makes it a promotion
criterion and connects it to the downstream computation.

\section{Ensemble Transport}
\label{sec:bf-highdim-ensemble-transport}

Ensemble transform methods build a coupling between forecast particles and
analysis particles.  Reich's ensemble transform method \citep{reich2013nonparametric}
and entropy-regularized optimal transport
\citep{villani2003topics,cuturi2013sinkhorn,peyre2019computational,schmitzer2019stabilized}
are useful because they expose a deterministic map or coupling matrix.  For
filtering, the approximation is the finite ensemble, the localization or map
class, the regularization, and the numerical transport solve.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Localized ensemble transport diagnostic step}
\label{alg:bf-highdim-ensemble-transport}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Forecast ensemble $\{x_f^{(i)}\}_{i=1}^N$, weights $\bar w_i$,
  localization blocks, transport regularization $\epsilon$.
\For{each localization block}
  \State Build a block cost matrix between forecast and analysis support.
  \State Solve a constrained coupling or entropy-regularized transport problem.
  \State Transform the block ensemble and record marginal mismatch.
\EndFor
\State Recombine blocks using the declared cross-block policy.
\State Record ESS before transport, coupling residual, localization residual,
  finite status, and posterior/reference diagnostics where available.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

For NAWM-like use, localization is the likely rescue structure.  Without it,
the transport problem itself becomes high-dimensional and finite-ensemble
collapse returns under a different name.

\section{Tensor-Train Density And Operator Compression Blocker}
\label{sec:bf-highdim-tt-filtering}

A tensor train represents a $d$-way array $A(i_1,\ldots,i_d)$ as
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bf-highdim-tt}
  A(i_1,\ldots,i_d)
  =
  G_1(i_1)G_2(i_2)\cdots G_d(i_d),
\end{equation}
where each $G_k(i_k)$ is an $r_{k-1}\times r_k$ matrix with $r_0=r_d=1$.
For equal mode size $M$ and rank bound $r$, storage is approximately
$O(dMr^2)$ rather than $O(M^d)$.  This is compression only if the ranks remain
small.

The filtering research idea is to tensorize a density, likelihood, transition
operator, observation map, or quadrature rule and propagate it through a
sequential update.  This execution does not have enough source support to present
that idea as an implementation recommendation.  The specific tensor-train
filtering papers identified in the May 27 plan remain metadata/source-gap
support until their technical sections are audited.  Therefore the only
review-ready content here is the quarantine contract: any future TT pilot must
expose rank growth, normalization error, positivity defects, and downstream
likelihood/score distortion before it can be used as more than a diagnostic.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Blocked tensor-train filtering pilot checklist}
\label{alg:bf-highdim-tt-pilot}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require A separately accepted source-review note, low-dimensional or
  block-structured fixture, tensorization map, initial TT approximation, rank cap
  $r_{\max}$, low-dimensional reference.
\State Stop if the source-review note is missing; do not implement from this
  chapter alone.
\For{$t=1,\ldots,T$}
  \State Apply the transition/update operator in TT form or project it into TT
    form.
  \State Round/truncate ranks with declared tolerance.
  \State Renormalize if the representation is a density.
  \State Record TT ranks, normalization residual, positivity residual, moment
    error, and value/score parity against the reference.
\EndFor
\State Keep the TT object quarantined as a diagnostic/proposal unless
  downstream posterior checks pass.
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Tensor-Network And Square-Root Blocker}
\label{sec:bf-highdim-tn-kalman}

Tensor-network Kalman filters are reported in the planning lane as a way to
compress lifted linear dynamics or covariance objects, but this run has only
metadata-level access to those sources.  Consequently this chapter does not make
a technical claim about tensor-network Kalman filtering.  It records a
BayesFilter blocker instead: if a future phase compresses covariance or lifted
dynamics, it must preserve an inspectable factor or square-root validity gate,
because direct rounding of covariance-like objects can lose positive
semidefiniteness.  That statement is a general numerical-safeguard requirement,
not evidence that the named tensor-network methods work for nonlinear SSMs.

The NeuroImage 2023 low-rank tensor UKF paper discussed in the planning lane is

\section{Industrial Practitioner Stress Test}
\label{sec:bf-highdim-ptt-industrial}

At NAWM-like scale, particle filters break first through support and weight
collapse.  Transport methods break when the map class cannot express the
posterior geometry or when Jacobian/density evaluation becomes unstable.  Tensor
methods break when ranks grow with shocks, regimes, or observation channels.
The only credible rescue is structure:
\begin{itemize}
  \item block-local proposals tied to economic model blocks;
  \item localization for ensemble transport;
  \item triangular or conditional transports whose density correction is cheap;
  \item tensor pilots restricted to source-reviewed maps or likelihood factors
    with measured low rank;
  \item downstream posterior checks before any method informs HMC or policy.
\end{itemize}

\section{What Is Not Concluded}
\label{sec:bf-highdim-ptt-nonclaims}

This chapter does not conclude that particle, flow, transport, tensor-train, or
tensor-network methods solve high-dimensional filtering.  It states the
corrections, diagnostics, and source gaps that must be closed before any method
can be treated as more than a candidate.
