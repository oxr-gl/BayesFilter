\section{The Filtering Problem In Its Least Forgiving Form}
\label{sec:bf-highdim-foundations-problem}

A high-dimensional nonlinear filter is not merely a large Kalman filter.  It is
an approximation to a sequence of conditional probability laws whose update
can become singular, multimodal, sharply curved, or numerically inaccessible.
This chapter fixes the mathematical objects that the later chapters approximate.
It also separates exact identities from approximation policies.  That separation
matters because a filter used inside optimization or Hamiltonian Monte Carlo
does not only produce state estimates; it defines a scalar likelihood target and
possibly a gradient of that target.

Let $\theta\in\Theta$ be static parameters, $x_t\in\R^n$ a latent state, and
$y_t\in\R^m$ an observation.  In discrete time a state-space model is
\begin{align}
  x_0 &\sim \mu_\theta, &
  x_t\mid x_{t-1},\theta &\sim f_\theta(\cdot\mid x_{t-1}), &
  y_t\mid x_t,\theta &\sim g_\theta(\cdot\mid x_t).
  \label{eq:bf-hd-ssm}
\end{align}
The continuous-time diffusion analogue, used by the Zakai/DMZ and tensor-train
PDE papers, is commonly written
\begin{align}
  dX_t &= a_\theta(X_t,t)\,dt + B_\theta(X_t,t)\,dV_t, \\
  dY_t &= h_\theta(X_t,t)\,dt + dW_t,
  \label{eq:bf-hd-continuous-ssm}
\end{align}
with variants for correlated noise.  The discrete recursion is the natural
contract for production filtering APIs; the continuous formulation exposes the
PDE and pathwise robust methods in \citet{davis1980multiplicative},
\citet{yau2000realtime}, \citet{yau2008realtime},
\citet{li2019ttfiltering}, and \citet{meng2025regularity}.

For a reader coming from numerical analysis, physics, or computational
chemistry, the central object is the conditional law
\(p_\theta(x_t\mid y_{1:t})\).  The input object at time \(t\) is the previous
conditional law and a new observation; the output object is a new conditional
law plus the normalizer \(Z_t\).  The exact target is the Bayes recursion below.
Every later method is an approximation to one of three objects: the law
\(p_\theta(x_t\mid y_{1:t})\), the likelihood scalar
\(\ell_T(\theta)\), or a derivative of that scalar.  A diagnostic is useful only
when it says which of these objects it is checking.

\paragraph{Running quadratic-observation cell.}
The same teaching example will be used through
Chapters~\ref{ch:bf-highdim-nonlinear-foundations}--\ref{ch:bf-highdim-candidate-synthesis}:
\begin{equation}
  x_t=\rho x_{t-1}+\eta_t,\qquad
  \eta_t\sim\calN(0,Q),\qquad
  y_t=x_t^2+\epsilon_t,\qquad
  \epsilon_t\sim\calN(0,R).
  \label{eq:bf-hd-running-cell}
\end{equation}
It is deliberately small.  Its purpose is to make the objects visible, not to
validate a macro-finance model.  With a one-step Gaussian prediction
\(X\sim\calN(0,P^-)\), the filtering density after observing \(y>0\) is
proportional to
\begin{equation}
  \exp\!\left\{-\frac{x^2}{2P^-}
  -\frac{(y-x^2)^2}{2R}\right\}.
  \label{eq:bf-hd-running-posterior}
\end{equation}
When \(R\) is small and \(y\) is away from zero, the likelihood rewards states
near both \(+\sqrt y\) and \(-\sqrt y\).  The exact filtering object is
therefore a conditional law that may have two lobes.  A mean alone, a covariance
alone, or a finite scalar likelihood alone is only one view of that object.

\begin{assumption}[Dominated filtering model]
\label{ass:bf-hd-dominated}
The kernels in \eqref{eq:bf-hd-ssm} admit densities with respect to reference
measures, the predictive normalizing constants below are finite and positive on
the observed path, and every differentiation under an integral sign is justified
by a local dominating function.  Continuous-time PDE statements additionally
use the regularity, ellipticity, growth, and boundary assumptions stated in the
cited source.
\end{assumption}

\section{Exact Discrete-Time Recursion And Likelihood}
\label{sec:bf-hd-exact-recursion}

Under Assumption~\ref{ass:bf-hd-dominated}, prediction and update are
\begin{align}
  p_\theta(x_t\mid y_{1:t-1})
    &= \int f_\theta(x_t\mid x_{t-1})
       p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid y_{1:t-1})\,dx_{t-1},
       \label{eq:bf-hd-prediction}\\
  p_\theta(x_t\mid y_{1:t})
    &= \frac{g_\theta(y_t\mid x_t)p_\theta(x_t\mid y_{1:t-1})}
            {Z_t(\theta)},\qquad
  Z_t(\theta)=\int g_\theta(y_t\mid x_t)
       p_\theta(x_t\mid y_{1:t-1})\,dx_t .
       \label{eq:bf-hd-update}
\end{align}
The likelihood factors as
\begin{equation}
  p_\theta(y_{1:T})=\prod_{t=1}^T Z_t(\theta),\qquad
  \ell_T(\theta)=\sum_{t=1}^T \log Z_t(\theta).
  \label{eq:bf-hd-likelihood-factor}
\end{equation}
The derivation is short, but it is the contract that every later approximation
must respect.  Write the joint density through time \(t\) as
\[
  p_\theta(x_{0:t},y_{1:t})
  =
  \mu_\theta(x_0)
  \prod_{s=1}^t f_\theta(x_s\mid x_{s-1})
  \prod_{s=1}^t g_\theta(y_s\mid x_s).
\]
Integrating out \(x_{0:t-1}\) after conditioning on \(y_{1:t-1}\) gives
\eqref{eq:bf-hd-prediction}.  Multiplying the predictive density by the
likelihood \(g_\theta(y_t\mid x_t)\) gives an unnormalized density
\[
  \gamma_t(x_t)=g_\theta(y_t\mid x_t)
       p_\theta(x_t\mid y_{1:t-1}).
\]
Its integral is exactly the one-step predictive density of the observation,
\[
  Z_t(\theta)=p_\theta(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1}).
\]
Bayes' formula then gives \eqref{eq:bf-hd-update}.  Finally, the chain rule for
the observation density gives
\[
  p_\theta(y_{1:T})
  =p_\theta(y_1)\prod_{t=2}^T p_\theta(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1})
  =\prod_{t=1}^T Z_t(\theta).
\]
Thus a filtering implementation that changes the normalizers changes the
likelihood, not merely the state estimate.

For actual stochastic-volatility rows, the target discipline is now explicit:
there is one cumulative likelihood target,
\(
\ell_T(\theta)=\sum_{t=1}^T\log Z_t(\theta)
\),
and every admissible numerical route must approximate that same scalar.  In the
transformed observation parameterization used in the code and later chapters,
\[
  z_t=\log(y_t^2),
  \qquad
  z_t-\log(\beta^2)-h_t\sim \log(\chi_1^2),
\]
so the one-step normalizer is defined by the exact transformed actual-SV
observation law.  Direct-likelihood quadrature, fixed-cloud reweighting, and the
current Zhao--Cui comparator are therefore comparable only because they are meant
to approximate that same normalizer sequence.

The previous actual-SV ``Lane~B'' framing was wrong for this inference problem.
It replaced the transformed actual-SV observation law by a Gaussian-closure
construction built from predictive observation moments of an augmented variable.
That replacement changes the implied one-step normalizer and therefore changes
the cumulative likelihood target itself.  A filter may still be internally
self-consistent for that replacement scalar, but it is not evidence about the
intended actual-SV likelihood in \eqref{eq:bf-hd-likelihood-factor}.  Any
implementation, benchmark, or gradient check must therefore keep two questions
separate: whether a method differentiates its own declared scalar correctly, and
whether that declared scalar is the right target in the first place.

\subsection{Actual-SV Single-Target Derivation}
\label{sec:bf-hd-actual-sv-single-target-derivation}

The corrected actual-SV statement can now be written as a short derivation.
Start from the raw observation equation
\begin{equation}
  y_t=\beta e^{h_t/2}\varepsilon_t,
  \qquad
  \varepsilon_t\sim \calN(0,1),
  \qquad
  \beta>0.
  \label{eq:bf-hd-actual-sv-raw-observation}
\end{equation}
For nonzero observations define the transformed datum
\begin{equation}
  z_t=\log(y_t^2).
  \label{eq:bf-hd-actual-sv-z-definition}
\end{equation}
Squaring \eqref{eq:bf-hd-actual-sv-raw-observation} gives
\(
  y_t^2=\beta^2 e^{h_t}\varepsilon_t^2
\), and taking logs yields
\begin{equation}
  z_t-\log(\beta^2)-h_t
  =
  \log(\varepsilon_t^2).
  \label{eq:bf-hd-actual-sv-transform-identity}
\end{equation}
Since \(\varepsilon_t^2\sim\chi_1^2\), the transformed observation density is
therefore
\begin{equation}
  g_\theta(z_t\mid h_t)
  =
  p_{\log\chi_1^2}\!\left(z_t-\log(\beta^2)-h_t\right).
  \label{eq:bf-hd-actual-sv-transform-density}
\end{equation}
Thus the transformed actual-SV one-step normalizer is
\begin{equation}
  Z_t(\theta)
  =
  \int g_\theta(z_t\mid h_t)
  p_\theta(h_t\mid z_{1:t-1})\,dh_t,
  \label{eq:bf-hd-actual-sv-normalizer}
\end{equation}
and the target remains the single cumulative likelihood
\(
  \ell_T(\theta)=\sum_{t=1}^T\log Z_t(\theta)
\).

This identity fixes the admissible same-target routes.  A dense exact-transformed
reference, a direct-likelihood fixed-cloud rule, or the current factorized
scalar Zhao--Cui wrapper may differ numerically, but they remain comparable only
when they all evaluate \eqref{eq:bf-hd-actual-sv-transform-density} and then
accumulate the resulting transformed normalizers.  The direct-likelihood route
has the generic node-reweighting form
\begin{equation}
  \widehat Z_t^{\rm dir}(\theta;B)
  =
  \sum_{r=1}^M
  w_r
g_\theta\bigl(z_t\mid h_t^{(r)}\bigr),
  \label{eq:bf-hd-actual-sv-direct-normalizer}
\end{equation}
with node locations \(h_t^{(r)}\) and weights \(w_r\) supplied by the declared
predictive approximation branch \(B\).  The dense reference and the present
Zhao--Cui comparator are same-target for the same reason: their value paths also
consume the transformed observations \(z_t\) and the exact transformed density
in \eqref{eq:bf-hd-actual-sv-transform-density}.

\begin{proposition}[Augmented Gaussian closure changes the actual-SV scalar]
\label{prop:bf-hd-actual-sv-gc-wrong-target}
Suppose instead that one augments the one-step state with an explicit
observation-noise coordinate and uses the raw observation map in
\eqref{eq:bf-hd-actual-sv-raw-observation}.  If the resulting filter propagates
that augmented cloud through the raw map, replaces
\eqref{eq:bf-hd-actual-sv-transform-density} by predictive observation moments,
and accumulates a Gaussian innovation likelihood such as
\eqref{eq:bf-hd-sgqf-loglik}, then the accumulated scalar is not the transformed
actual-SV likelihood target \(\ell_T(\theta)\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The transformed target in \eqref{eq:bf-hd-actual-sv-normalizer} is defined by
integrating the exact transformed observation density
\(g_\theta(z_t\mid h_t)\).  By contrast, the augmented Gaussian-closure route
uses the raw map \(y_t=\beta e^{h_t/2}\varepsilon_t\), forms predictive
observation moments such as \(\bar z_t\) and \(S_t\), and then accumulates a
Gaussian innovation scalar.  That replacement changes the observation-density
object inside the one-step normalizer.  Once the one-step normalizer changes, the
cumulative likelihood changes as well by \eqref{eq:bf-hd-likelihood-factor}.
Hence the Gaussian-closure route may define a different declared approximate
scalar, but it is not a same-target approximation to the transformed actual-SV
likelihood.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[When a Gaussian-closure likelihood surrogate can be reasonable]
\label{prop:bf-hd-gaussian-closure-surrogate-regime}
Let \(p_t^{\rm obs}(y)\) be the predictive observation density induced by the
correct augmented input law, and let \((\bar z_t,S_t)\) be the predictive mean
and covariance used in the Gaussian innovation scalar
\eqref{eq:bf-hd-sgqf-loglik}.  Suppose:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*), leftmargin=0.28in]
  \item the sigma-point or fixed-cloud rule is applied to the correct augmented
    input variable, so that the moment statement in
    Proposition~\ref{prop:bf-structural-ukf-ut-accuracy} applies;
  \item the local small-uncertainty and smoothness hypotheses behind
    Proposition~\ref{prop:bf-structural-ukf-ut-accuracy} hold, so the predictive
    mean and covariance are accurate through the stated second-order regime; and
  \item on a neighborhood \(\mathcal U_t\) containing the realized observation
    \(y_t\), the predictive observation log density satisfies
    \begin{equation}
      \log p_t^{\rm obs}(y)
      =
      \log \varphi_{n_y}(y;\bar z_t,S_t)+r_t(y),
      \qquad y\in\mathcal U_t,
      \label{eq:bf-hd-gaussian-closure-log-density-remainder}
    \end{equation}
    with remainder bounded by
    \begin{equation}
      |r_t(y)|\le \delta_t,
      \qquad y\in\mathcal U_t.
      \label{eq:bf-hd-gaussian-closure-remainder-bound}
    \end{equation}
\end{enumerate}
Then, provided \(y_t\in\mathcal U_t\), the one-step Gaussian innovation scalar
is a local likelihood surrogate with error bounded by
\begin{equation}
  \left|
    \log p_t^{\rm obs}(y_t)
    -
    \left[-\frac12\left\{\log\det S_t+v_t^\top S_t^{-1}v_t+n_y\log(2\pi)\right\}\right]
  \right|
  \le \delta_t.
  \label{eq:bf-hd-gaussian-closure-surrogate-bound}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By definition of the Gaussian density,
\[
  \log \varphi_{n_y}(y_t;\bar z_t,S_t)
  =
  -\frac12\left\{\log\det S_t+v_t^\top S_t^{-1}v_t+n_y\log(2\pi)\right\},
\]
with \(v_t=y_t-\bar z_t\).  Evaluating
\eqref{eq:bf-hd-gaussian-closure-log-density-remainder} at \(y_t\) gives
\[
  \log p_t^{\rm obs}(y_t)
  -
  \log \varphi_{n_y}(y_t;\bar z_t,S_t)
  =
  r_t(y_t),
\]
and \eqref{eq:bf-hd-gaussian-closure-surrogate-bound} follows from
\eqref{eq:bf-hd-gaussian-closure-remainder-bound}.
\end{proof}

This proposition states the admissible sense in which a UKF-style or
fixed-cloud Gaussian closure can still be useful.  The structural UKF result
controls predictive moments under the correct augmented input law; the extra
remainder assumption controls how far the full predictive observation density is
from the Gaussian density built from those moments.  Both ingredients are needed.
Accurate moments alone do not guarantee an accurate likelihood scalar when the
predictive observation law is strongly skewed, heavy-tailed, multimodal, or
otherwise far from Gaussian near the realized observation.

For actual SV this means the augmented Gaussian-closure route may be a reasonable
local surrogate likelihood under a small-uncertainty, near-Gaussian predictive
observation regime, but it still does not become the same target as the
transformed exact likelihood in \eqref{eq:bf-hd-actual-sv-normalizer}.  It is a
surrogate scalar, not a second exact route to the same inference problem.

The practical consequence is strict.  Same-scalar derivative checks, fixed-branch
audits, or optimizer/HMC experiments are meaningful only after the value path is
a route to \eqref{eq:bf-hd-actual-sv-normalizer}.  Internal consistency of a
Gaussian-closure scalar does not repair the target mismatch.  This is why the
current transformed direct-likelihood route and the current Zhao--Cui comparator
remain admissible same-target comparators, whereas the previous augmented
Gaussian-closure framing does not.

\paragraph{Reader checkpoint.}
What should now be clear: filtering updates a probability law and a scalar
normalizer at the same time.  Object preserved: the conditional law after
normalization.  Approximation enters at: the representation of the predictive
law, the integral for \(Z_t\), or both.  Exported to synthesis: any industrial
method must say whether it preserves the intended likelihood scalar, merely a
different surrogate scalar, or only a moment summary.

\begin{proposition}[Predictive score identity]
\label{prop:bf-hd-score}
Let $Z_t(\theta)=\int a_t(x,\theta)\,dx$ with
$a_t(x,\theta)=g_\theta(y_t\mid x)p_\theta(x\mid y_{1:t-1})$.  If
$a_t>0$ on the posterior support and dominated differentiation holds, then
\begin{equation}
  \nabla_\theta\log Z_t(\theta)
  =
  \int \nabla_\theta\log a_t(x,\theta)\,
       p_\theta(x\mid y_{1:t})\,dx .
  \label{eq:bf-hd-score}
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Under dominated differentiation,
\[
  \nabla_\theta\log Z_t(\theta)
  =\frac{\nabla_\theta Z_t(\theta)}{Z_t(\theta)}
  =
  \frac{\int \nabla_\theta a_t(x,\theta)\,dx}
       {\int a_t(x,\theta)\,dx}.
\]
On the support where \(a_t>0\),
\(\nabla_\theta a_t=a_t\nabla_\theta\log a_t\).  Substitution gives
\[
  \nabla_\theta\log Z_t(\theta)
  =
  \int \nabla_\theta\log a_t(x,\theta)
       \frac{a_t(x,\theta)}{Z_t(\theta)}\,dx .
\]
The last factor is the filtering density in
\eqref{eq:bf-hd-update}.  Hence \eqref{eq:bf-hd-score} follows.  The identity
does not say that a numerical score is correct; it says that any reported score
must correspond to the derivative of the same normalizer used in the reported
likelihood.
\end{proof}

This proposition is exact but not automatically implementable.  A numerical
filter supplies an approximate normalizer $\widehat Z_t$ and an approximate
history-dependent distribution.  If value and gradient are not derivatives of
the same scalar approximation, a downstream optimizer or HMC sampler is solving
a different problem from the one reported by the filter.

\section{Analytical Likelihood-Gradient And Filtering Sensitivities}
\label{sec:bf-hd-analytical-likelihood-gradient}

This section is a project derivation for the dominated discrete-time model in
\eqref{eq:bf-hd-ssm}.  It is not a continuous-time adjoint theorem.  The PDE
and robust-DMZ settings require their own sensitivity or adjoint analysis under
the regularity assumptions of the cited sources.

Write
\[
  p_t^-(x_t)=p_\theta(x_t\mid y_{1:t-1}),\qquad
  p_t(x_t)=p_\theta(x_t\mid y_{1:t}),
\]
and let the parameter-gradient sensitivities be
\[
  s_t^-(x_t)=\nabla_\theta p_t^-(x_t),\qquad
  s_t(x_t)=\nabla_\theta p_t(x_t).
\]
These sensitivities are vector-valued when \(\theta\) is vector-valued; the
derivation is componentwise.  Differentiating the prediction equation
\eqref{eq:bf-hd-prediction} under Assumption~\ref{ass:bf-hd-dominated} gives
\begin{equation}
  s^-_t(x_t)
  =
  \int
  \left[
  \nabla_\theta f_\theta(x_t\mid x_{t-1})
  p_{t-1}(x_{t-1})
  +
  f_\theta(x_t\mid x_{t-1})
  s_{t-1}(x_{t-1})
  \right]dx_{t-1}.
  \label{eq:bf-hd-prediction-sensitivity}
\end{equation}
The first term is the direct parameter effect on the transition density.  The
second term is the inherited sensitivity of the previous filtering law.  If
the initial distribution depends on \(\theta\), the recursion starts from
\(s_0(x_0)=\nabla_\theta p_\theta(x_0)\), or from the corresponding
post-initial-observation sensitivity if an initial update is used.

The one-step normalizer is
\begin{equation}
  Z_t(\theta)=\int g_\theta(y_t\mid x_t)p_t^-(x_t)\,dx_t .
  \label{eq:bf-hd-gradient-normalizer}
\end{equation}
Differentiating it gives
\begin{equation}
  \nabla_\theta Z_t(\theta)
  =
  \int
  \left[
  \nabla_\theta g_\theta(y_t\mid x_t)p^-_t(x_t)
  +
  g_\theta(y_t\mid x_t)s^-_t(x_t)
  \right]dx_t .
  \label{eq:bf-hd-normalizer-gradient}
\end{equation}
Therefore
\begin{equation}
  \nabla_\theta \ell_T(\theta)
  =
  \sum_{t=1}^T
  \nabla_\theta \log Z_t(\theta)
  =
  \sum_{t=1}^T
  \frac{\nabla_\theta Z_t(\theta)}{Z_t(\theta)} .
  \label{eq:bf-hd-likelihood-gradient}
\end{equation}

It remains to differentiate the normalized update.  Define
\[
  n_t(x_t,\theta)=g_\theta(y_t\mid x_t)p_t^-(x_t),
  \qquad
  p_t(x_t)=\frac{n_t(x_t,\theta)}{Z_t(\theta)}.
\]
The numerator sensitivity is
\[
  \nabla_\theta n_t(x_t,\theta)
  =
  \nabla_\theta g_\theta(y_t\mid x_t)p_t^-(x_t)
  +
  g_\theta(y_t\mid x_t)s_t^-(x_t).
\]
Using the quotient rule and
\(\nabla_\theta Z_t/Z_t=\nabla_\theta\log Z_t\),
\begin{equation}
  s_t(x_t)
  =
  \frac{
  \nabla_\theta g_\theta(y_t\mid x_t)p^-_t(x_t)
  +
  g_\theta(y_t\mid x_t)s^-_t(x_t)
  }{Z_t(\theta)}
  -
  p_t(x_t)\nabla_\theta\log Z_t(\theta).
  \label{eq:bf-hd-update-sensitivity}
\end{equation}
Integrating \eqref{eq:bf-hd-update-sensitivity} over \(x_t\) gives zero,
because a normalized density has derivative of total mass equal to zero.  This
is the sensitivity analogue of probability conservation.

\begin{proposition}[Discrete-time likelihood sensitivity recursion]
\label{prop:bf-hd-likelihood-sensitivity}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:bf-hd-dominated}, with differentiable transition and
observation densities and finite positive \(Z_t(\theta)\), equations
\eqref{eq:bf-hd-prediction-sensitivity}--\eqref{eq:bf-hd-update-sensitivity}
give an analytical recursion for \(\nabla_\theta\ell_T(\theta)\) in the exact
discrete-time filter.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Equation~\eqref{eq:bf-hd-prediction-sensitivity} is differentiation of the
prediction integral.  Equation~\eqref{eq:bf-hd-normalizer-gradient} is
differentiation of the predictive normalizer.  Equation
\eqref{eq:bf-hd-likelihood-gradient} follows from the likelihood factorization
\eqref{eq:bf-hd-likelihood-factor} and the identity
\(\nabla\log Z=\nabla Z/Z\) for \(Z>0\).  Equation
\eqref{eq:bf-hd-update-sensitivity} is the quotient rule applied to
\(p_t=n_t/Z_t\).  Each step is componentwise in \(\theta\) and uses dominated
differentiation to exchange gradient and integral.
\end{proof}

This recursion is the analytical object needed by Chapter~\ref{ch:bf-highdim-hmc-research}.
An automatic-differentiation tape through an approximate filter is a different
object unless it differentiates exactly the same scalar displayed to the
sampler.

\paragraph{Reader checkpoint.}
What should now be clear: the likelihood gradient is not a separate numerical
decoration.  It is the derivative of the same normalizers that define
\(\ell_T\).  Object preserved: scalar value-gradient consistency.  Approximation
enters at: any approximate \(p_t^-\), \(\widehat Z_t\), truncation, rounding, or
adaptive gate.  Exported to synthesis: HMC can start only after the scalar and
gradient contract is named.

\section{Zakai, Kushner--Stratonovich, And DMZ Equations}
\label{sec:bf-hd-zakai-dmz}

For diffusion models, the normalized conditional distribution satisfies a
nonlinear filtering equation, while the unnormalized conditional measure
satisfies a linear Zakai equation.  We use \citet{vanhandel2007filtering} as the
standard checked derivation anchor for the normalized and unnormalized
equations rather than relying on unchecked historical priority papers.  In an
informal density notation, the unnormalized density $\sigma_t$ obeys a linear
stochastic equation of the form
\begin{equation}
  d\sigma_t
  =
  \mathcal L_\theta^\ast \sigma_t\,dt
  + h_\theta^\top \sigma_t\,dY_t,
  \label{eq:bf-hd-zakai-density}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal L_\theta^\ast$ is the Fokker--Planck adjoint associated with the
signal generator.  The normalized density is
\begin{equation}
  \rho_t(x)=\frac{\sigma_t(x)}{\int \sigma_t(u)\,du}.
  \label{eq:bf-hd-normalize-zakai}
\end{equation}
The Duncan--Mortensen--Zakai viewpoint emphasizes the density/PDE form of
\eqref{eq:bf-hd-zakai-density}.  It is attractive in high dimension because it
turns filtering into density propagation; it is frightening for exactly the same
reason, since a tensor-product grid has exponentially many degrees of freedom.

\begin{remark}[Bibliographic scope]
For the equations used below, this chapter relies on checked technical sources:
van Handel for the standard normalized and unnormalized filtering equations, and
Davis/Yau papers for the pathwise robust DMZ transformation and memoryless
algorithm.  Classical priority names are retained for orientation, but a
historical citation is not used as a substitute for an inspected theorem or
derivation.
\end{remark}

\section{Pathwise Robust DMZ And Memoryless Computation}
\label{sec:bf-hd-pathwise-dmz}

\citet{davis1980multiplicative} studies a multiplicative functional
transformation for nonlinear filtering.  In the simple scalar-observation
diffusion setting, the idea is to absorb observation-path dependence into a
path-dependent transformation of the unnormalized density.  The transformed
object evolves according to a generator/PDE that can be studied pathwise rather
than only as a stochastic differential equation.

\citet{yau2000realtime} and \citet{yau2008realtime} build a robust DMZ
algorithm around this principle.  Their central computational move is to
freeze observation increments on a time partition and repeatedly solve
observation-independent Kolmogorov-type equations, correcting by
observation-dependent multiplicative factors.  In schematic form, over
$[t_j,t_{j+1}]$ one computes
\begin{align}
  \partial_s u_j(s,x) &= \mathcal K_{y_{t_j}} u_j(s,x),
       \qquad s\in[t_j,t_{j+1}], \label{eq:bf-hd-frozen-kolmogorov}\\
  u_{j+1}(t_{j+1},x) &=
       \mathcal M(y_{t_{j+1}}-y_{t_j},x)\,u_j(t_{j+1},x),
       \label{eq:bf-hd-memoryless-update}
\end{align}
where $\mathcal K_{y_{t_j}}$ and $\mathcal M$ denote the paper-specific frozen
operator and multiplicative observation update.  The details are not optional:
the convergence and bounded-domain approximation theorems in
\citet{yau2000realtime,yau2008realtime} depend on their growth, regularity,
weak-solution, and boundary assumptions.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Robust DMZ offline/online template}
\label{alg:bf-hd-robust-dmz}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Partition $0=t_0<\cdots<t_K=T$, initial density, checked PR-DMZ
  assumptions, spatial domain or truncation rule.
\State Build or choose the observation-independent Kolmogorov solver required by
  the cited theorem.
\For{$j=0,\ldots,K-1$}
  \State Freeze the observation path according to the cited rule.
  \State Solve the frozen Kolmogorov equation on $[t_j,t_{j+1}]$.
  \State Apply the multiplicative observation correction.
  \State Normalize only after preserving the unnormalized-density semantics.
  \State Record mass, positivity, boundary, and discretization residuals.
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\citet{meng2025regularity} is a recent arXiv contribution rather than a settled
classical reference.  It is therefore used here provisionally: it links pathwise
robust DMZ regularity estimates to sparse functional tensor-train
approximations and a finite-difference QTT algorithm.  The durable lesson for
the rest of the monograph is methodological rather than evidentiary: a tensor
filter must make regularity, rank/compressibility, discretization error,
rounding error, and online-assimilation stability visible before small storage
can be interpreted as a filtering result.

\section{Approximate Targets And The HMC Boundary}
\label{sec:bf-hd-approx-target}

For a prior $\pi(\theta)$, the exact parameter posterior is proportional to
\begin{equation}
  \pi(\theta\mid y_{1:T})
  \propto \exp\{\ell_T(\theta)\}\pi(\theta).
  \label{eq:bf-hd-exact-posterior}
\end{equation}
If a filter supplies $\widehat\ell_T(\theta)$, the posterior implied by the
calculation is
\begin{equation}
  \widehat\pi(\theta\mid y_{1:T})
  \propto \exp\{\widehat\ell_T(\theta)\}\pi(\theta),
  \label{eq:bf-hd-approx-posterior}
\end{equation}
unless a pseudo-marginal or other exact correction is part of the algorithm.
The corresponding gradient is
\[
  \nabla_\theta \widehat\ell_T(\theta)
  =
  \nabla_\theta\sum_{t=1}^T\log\widehat Z_t(\theta),
\]
provided the reported gradient is the derivative of the reported scalar.
Truncation, resampling, clipping, tensor rounding, adaptive stopping, and
discrete accept/reject gates must therefore state which scalar is being
differentiated.  The HMC chapter returns to this point: exact Hamiltonian
dynamics for \eqref{eq:bf-hd-approx-posterior} is not exact sampling from
\eqref{eq:bf-hd-exact-posterior}.

\section{Why Dimension Breaks Filters}
\label{sec:bf-hd-failure-modes}

High dimension is a family of failure mechanisms.

\begin{description}[leftmargin=*, style=nextline]
  \item[Particle collapse.]
  The bootstrap and SIR filters of \citet{gordon1993novel} and
  \citet{arulampalam2002tutorial} represent the filtering law by particles and
  weights.  \citet{bengtsson2008curse} and \citet{snyder2008obstacles} show
  that, under their assumptions, weight concentration can be governed by the
  variance of the log likelihood and can require enormous ensembles.  These
  results do not say every structured particle method fails; they say proposal,
  localization, and correction must be central.

  \item[Grid explosion.]
  A density grid with $M$ points per coordinate has $M^n$ entries.  Sparse grids,
  tensor trains, and transport maps are attempts to exploit smoothness,
  low-order interactions, low rank, or triangular structure.  If the posterior
  lacks that structure, the format fails.

  \item[Gaussian projection bias.]
  EKF/UKF/CKF-style filters propagate only selected moments.  Multimodality,
  skewness, sharp likelihoods, and nonlocal curvature can be invisible to the
  chosen moment rule.

  \item[Sampler geometry.]
  HMC and NUTS can fail through divergences, poor energy exploration, invalid
  regions, or a value/gradient mismatch even when the filter returns finite
  numbers.
\end{description}

\section{Defects Passed To Synthesis}
\label{sec:bf-hd-foundations-defects}

This chapter passes five defects to the synthesis chapter.

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
  \item The exact object is a sequence of probability laws and normalizers.
  A later method that returns a mean but not a defensible \(Z_t\) has not
  supplied a likelihood target for parameter inference.
  \item The approximate posterior in \eqref{eq:bf-hd-approx-posterior} is a
  different target unless an exact correction is part of the method.  This is
  the first same-scalar obligation inherited by HMC.
  \item Analytical score recursions such as
  \eqref{eq:bf-hd-likelihood-gradient} are available in the dominated
  discrete-time setting.  Continuous-time/PDE score and adjoint formulas are
  not derived here and remain source-specific obligations.
  \item Density/PDE formulations require mass, positivity, boundary, and
  regularity checks.  A low residual in a transformed PDE does not by itself
  prove that the normalized density is a valid filtering law.
  \item Source-local PDE assumptions matter.  The Davis and Yau robust-DMZ
  transformations and the Meng tensor approximation results support the
  statements made under their hypotheses; they do not license an unrestricted
  high-dimensional filtering theorem for BayesFilter.
\end{enumerate}

The synthesis chapter uses these defects as gates: exact target first,
approximation target second, sampler or acceleration only downstream.

\section{Methodological Boundary}
\label{sec:bf-hd-foundations-boundary}

The chapters that follow are literature-grounded design chapters, not production
promotion notes.  A numerical smoke test can show that a command ran, values
were finite, shapes matched, or a stop rule triggered.  It cannot establish
posterior accuracy, NAWM readiness, tensor validation, HMC convergence, GPU/XLA
readiness, or a default production policy.

\section{Bibliographic Limits}

Several historical or specialized sources were not available for direct
technical inspection in this pass: Savostyanov-specific maxvol/quasioptimality,
