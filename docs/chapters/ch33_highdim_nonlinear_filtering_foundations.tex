This chapter fixes the notation, exact identities, and evidence boundaries used

Literature statements in this block use four support classes:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item \emph{primary technical source}: a cited paper/book is used for a
    specific method or diagnostic claim;
  \item \emph{local ResearchAssistant summary}: the local MCP summary is used
    only as review material and never as theorem-level support;
  \item \emph{BayesFilter artifact}: a code path, benchmark row, result note, or
    source-map entry supports only the exact observed engineering fact;
  \item \emph{source-gap blocker}: the idea is named because it matters to the
    research program, but the present chapter refuses to use it as support for
    a technical claim.
\end{enumerate}
This convention is part of the content, not clerical metadata: it prevents a
survey sentence from becoming an accidental validation claim.

\section{State-Space Contract}
\label{sec:bf-highdim-ssm-contract}

Let $\theta \in \Theta$ be static parameters, $x_t\in\mathbb R^n$ a latent
state, and $y_t\in\mathbb R^m$ an observation.  A discrete-time nonlinear
state-space model is specified by an initial law $\mu_\theta(dx_0)$, transition
kernel $f_\theta(dx_t\mid x_{t-1})$, and observation likelihood
$g_\theta(y_t\mid x_t)$:
\begin{align}
  x_0 &\sim \mu_\theta, &
  x_t \mid x_{t-1},\theta &\sim f_\theta(\cdot\mid x_{t-1}), &
  y_t \mid x_t,\theta &\sim g_\theta(\cdot\mid x_t).
  \label{eq:bf-highdim-ssm-kernels}
\end{align}
When simulation maps exist, we write
\begin{align}
  x_t &= F_\theta(x_{t-1},\varepsilon_t), &
  y_t &= H_\theta(x_t,\eta_t),
  \label{eq:bf-highdim-simulation-form}
\end{align}
with innovations $\varepsilon_t$ and observation shocks $\eta_t$.  In structural
models the full state may contain deterministic completion coordinates.  The
dimension that controls particles or quadrature is then the declared stochastic
integration dimension, not merely the length of the storage vector.

\begin{assumption}[Existence and dominated differentiation]
\label{ass:bf-highdim-existence}
For exact filtering identities, the transition and observation kernels are
defined on a common measurable state space, the predictive densities appearing
below are finite and positive on the observed path, and all integrals are with
respect to the model-induced measure.  For score identities, differentiation
under the integral sign is justified by a local dominating function.
\end{assumption}

\section{Prediction, Update, And Likelihood}
\label{sec:bf-highdim-prediction-update}

\begin{definition}[Exact filtering recursion]
\label{def:bf-highdim-filtering-recursion}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:bf-highdim-existence}, the prediction and update
density recursions are
\begin{align}
  p_\theta(x_t \mid y_{1:t-1})
    &= \int f_\theta(x_t \mid x_{t-1})
       p_\theta(x_{t-1}\mid y_{1:t-1})\,dx_{t-1},
       \label{eq:bf-highdim-prediction}\\
  p_\theta(x_t \mid y_{1:t})
    &= \frac{g_\theta(y_t\mid x_t)p_\theta(x_t\mid y_{1:t-1})}
            {p_\theta(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1})},
       \label{eq:bf-highdim-update}
\end{align}
where
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bf-highdim-predictive-density}
  p_\theta(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1})
    = \int g_\theta(y_t\mid x_t)p_\theta(x_t\mid y_{1:t-1})\,dx_t.
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}[Likelihood factorization]
\label{prop:bf-highdim-likelihood-factorization}
If the conditional predictive densities in
\eqref{eq:bf-highdim-predictive-density} exist, are finite, and are positive,
then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bf-highdim-loglik-factorization}
  \ell_T(\theta)
  =
  \log p_\theta(y_{1:T})
  =
  \sum_{t=1}^T \log p_\theta(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1}).
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The probability chain rule gives
$p_\theta(y_{1:T})=\prod_{t=1}^T p_\theta(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1})$.  Positivity and
finiteness permit taking logarithms term by term.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[Predictive-score identity]
\label{prop:bf-highdim-predictive-score}
Let $Z_t(\theta)=p_\theta(y_t\mid y_{1:t-1})$ be finite and positive, and
assume dominated differentiation.  Then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bf-highdim-predictive-score}
  \nabla_\theta \log Z_t(\theta)
  =
  \frac{\nabla_\theta Z_t(\theta)}{Z_t(\theta)}.
\end{equation}
If $Z_t(\theta)=\int a_t(x_t,\theta)\,dx_t$ for
$a_t(x_t,\theta)=g_\theta(y_t\mid x_t)p_\theta(x_t\mid y_{1:t-1})$, then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bf-highdim-score-integral}
  \nabla_\theta \log Z_t(\theta)
  =
  \int \nabla_\theta \log a_t(x_t,\theta)\,
       p_\theta(x_t\mid y_{1:t})\,dx_t,
\end{equation}
whenever $a_t>0$ on the posterior support.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Equation~\eqref{eq:bf-highdim-predictive-score} is the derivative of
$\log Z_t$.  For \eqref{eq:bf-highdim-score-integral}, dominated
differentiation gives
$\nabla_\theta Z_t=\int \nabla_\theta a_t\,dx_t
=\int a_t\nabla_\theta\log a_t\,dx_t$.  Divide by $Z_t$ and use
$a_t/Z_t=p_\theta(x_t\mid y_{1:t})$ from \eqref{eq:bf-highdim-update}.
\end{proof}

The score identity is not an implementation instruction by itself.  In a large
nonlinear SSM the derivative of the predictive density also depends on the
filtering distribution inherited from the previous step.  A backend must state
which approximation it differentiates and must verify that the scalar value and
gradient refer to the same approximate target.

\section{Exact Posterior Versus Approximate Posterior}
\label{sec:bf-highdim-approximate-targets}

For a parameter prior $\pi(\theta)$, the exact parameter posterior is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bf-highdim-posterior-target}
  \log \pi(\theta\mid y_{1:T})
  =
  \ell_T(\theta) + \log \pi(\theta) - \log p(y_{1:T}).
\end{equation}
A nonlinear filter used inside HMC or optimization usually supplies
$\widehat\ell_T(\theta)$ instead.  Unless an unbiased pseudo-marginal or
otherwise target-correcting mechanism is supplied, the resulting target is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bf-highdim-approx-posterior}
  \widehat\pi(\theta\mid y_{1:T})
  \propto
  \exp\{\widehat\ell_T(\theta)\}\pi(\theta),
\end{equation}
not the exact posterior in \eqref{eq:bf-highdim-posterior-target}.  This is the
main boundary behind the later HMC chapter: an exact integrator for an
approximate likelihood samples the approximate posterior.

\section{High-Dimensional Failure Mechanisms}
\label{sec:bf-highdim-degeneracy}

High dimension is not a single obstacle.  It is a collection of failure modes
that show up in different ledgers:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Gaussian projection}: the propagated covariance can lose positive
    semidefiniteness, the local linearization can be wrong in the posterior
    mass, or a low-order moment closure can hide multimodality.
  \item \emph{Quadrature}: global tensor rules grow exponentially, and
    high-degree cubature may spend points on interactions that the model does
    not actually have.
  \item \emph{Particles}: normalized weights
    $\bar w_i=w_i/\sum_jw_j$ can concentrate, giving
    $\mathrm{ESS}=1/\sum_i\bar w_i^2$ close to one even with many particles;
    this is the obstacle emphasized in high-dimensional particle-filter
    analyses such as \citet{bengtsson2008curse} and \citet{snyder2008obstacles}.
  \item \emph{HMC}: finite log density and gradients do not guarantee valid
    exploration.  Divergences, poor energy exploration, high $\widehat R$, low
    effective sample size, and strong step-size sensitivity can veto a chain
    before speed is meaningful; see the HMC diagnostics framing of
    \citet{neal2011mcmc} and \citet{betancourt2017conceptual}.
  \item \emph{Tensor compression}: rank truncation can reduce storage while
    introducing signed-density artifacts, normalization drift, covariance
    invalidity, or posterior distortion.
\end{itemize}

\section{NAWM-Like Industrial Stress}
\label{sec:bf-highdim-nawm-stress}

The NAWM-style nonlinear DSGE target is used here as a stress standard, not as
validation evidence.  A method that looks plausible on a ten-dimensional
fixture can fail at NAWM-like scale because the state vector mixes stocks,
forward-looking controls, shocks, measurement channels, occasionally binding
features, deterministic completion equations, and parameter transforms.  The
three rescue structures that matter most are:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
  \item \emph{locality}: economic blocks or shock blocks interact sparsely;
  \item \emph{low rank}: observation or transition sensitivity is concentrated
    in a smaller active subspace;
  \item \emph{transport geometry}: an invertible or approximately invertible
    map removes the worst curvature before filtering or sampling.
\end{enumerate}
Each is a hypothesis.  Promotion requires evidence on the downstream filtering
or posterior computation, not only a local residual, a training loss, or a
small smoke row.

\section{Ledgers And Promotion Rule}
\label{sec:bf-highdim-ledgers}

The remaining chapters keep five ledgers:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item engineering correctness: contracts, shapes, dtypes, finite status, and
    reproducible commands;
  \item numerical validity: parity against a declared comparator, positive
    covariance factors, and stable residuals;
  \item sampler validity: divergences, acceptance, $\widehat R$, ESS, E-BFMI,
    energy error, and posterior/reference checks under a stated sampler plan;
  \item scientific interpretation: whether the approximation answers the
    statistical or economic question;
  \item performance evidence: runtime, memory, compilation, and device behavior
    after validity vetoes have passed.
\end{enumerate}
No result moves from one ledger to another without an explicit promotion
criterion.  A fast invalid row is invalid first and fast second.
